Section 1

Glue, Adhesive, or Cement?

Understanding what the words actually mean

Glue

The oldest and loosest term. Technically refers to adhesives made from organic materials like animal hides or fish. In modern usage it has become a catch-all word for anything that sticks things together. When a customer says "model glue," that tells you nothing about what they actually need.

Adhesive

The correct technical umbrella term for any substance that bonds two surfaces together by chemical or mechanical means. CA, epoxy, E6000, and PVA are all adhesives. This is the most accurate word for the category as a whole.

Cement (solvent)

In the hobby world, cement refers specifically to a solvent-based product that works by dissolving the surfaces and fusing them together. There is no glue layer added. The joint becomes one unified material, not two pieces stuck together. Plastic cement is the classic example.

Why It Matters

Recommending cement to a customer working with resin or foam is a mistake. Recommending CA to a styrene modeler when solvent cement would give a cleaner seam is a missed opportunity.

The follow-up question is always: What material are you bonding?

Knowledge Check

A customer walks in and asks for model glue to fix their plastic model airplane. Before recommending anything, what is the single most important follow-up question?

The material determines everything. Styrene uses solvent cement. Resin, foam, and rubber need an adhesive like CA. Recommending the wrong product is the most common floor mistake.
Section 2

Cyanoacrylate (CA)

{Cy-ano-ack-relate} — The most versatile adhesive in the hobby world

What It Is and How It Works

What it is
A fast-curing acrylic resin adhesive. In hobby shops it typically comes in three viscosities: thin (water-like), medium, and thick (gel)

How it works
CA cures through anionic polymerization triggered by trace moisture in the air or on the surface — it's not evaporation-based like wood glue. This is why it bonds so fast (seconds) and why it doesn't work well in very dry environments without an accelerator (kicker) spray.

Key rules

  • Less is more. A thin, even application beats a big glob every time. Excess CA causes white fogging, weakens joints, and makes a mess.
  • CA bonds to skin near instantly, so be extra careful.
  • Regular CA will "eat" foam.

Viscosity Options — tap to expand

Thin CA
Cure: 5-15 seconds
Water-like. Wicks into tight joints by capillary action. Place parts first, then apply.
Medium CA
Cure: 15-30 sec
Light syrup. More working time. Fills minor gaps. Good general-purpose choice.
Thick CA
Cure: 30-60 sec
Honey-like. Stays where placed. Significant gap fill. Good for rough or porous surfaces.
Gel CA
Cure: 30-90 sec
Non-drip, no-run. Maximum gap fill. For vertical surfaces and foam.

Odorless CA

▾
Same bond strength but produces less fume. Ideal for customers sensitive to fumes or working indoors in small spaces.

As a bonus, Odorless CA is safe to use on foam models.

Flexible CA

▾
Cures with flex rather than brittle hardness. For rubber, vinyl, and joints that absorb vibration.

RC Tire Glue

▾
Thin CA formulated specifically for bonding to the hard nylon of RC car wheels.

Accelerator (Insta-Set)

▾
Spray or brush-on chemical that cures CA instantly. Use sparingly — too much causes white blush on clear parts and can weaken the joint.

Debonder (Un-Cure)

▾
Dissolves cured CA. Fixes mistakes, separates bonded parts, and removes CA from skin.

Storing CA

▾

Exposure to heat and moisture are the two main factors in decreasing the shelf life of CA. If kept at normal room temperature (74°F) and humidity levels, BSI's regular CAs will have a shelf life of over two years. Unopened bottles can be stored in a freezer, but this offers little or no advantage over storage in a refrigerator. CA bottles can be stored in a sealed container inside the refrigerator to isolate them from moisture. Adding a desiccant (silica gel) to the container can extend the life of the CA to over four years. Opened bottles that are frequently used should not be put into the fridge since condensation can occur inside the bottle. Accelerator should never be stored with CA.

The first important step is to make a clean cut with a razor blade or hobby knife when you first cut off the tip of the nozzle. When you are done using the bottle, sit it down hard and then squeeze the bottle. Wipe the nozzle clean of any excess CA, then squeeze the bottle again to verify you can hear air blowing through it. If you look straight down at the tip (not while squeezing it!) you should see a black dot. That means the nozzle is clear and you can put the colored cap back on. If a buildup of CA occurs at the tip, many times you can clear it just by using your fingernails to remove the cured CA.

—Bob Smith Industries

Knowledge Check

A customer is building a foam RC airplane and needs to glue foam panels together. Which CA product is the right recommendation?

Standard CA melts foam on contact. Odorless CA — Super Gold or Super Gold+ — is foam-safe. This is a must-know distinction. Sending someone home with regular CA for a foam build will ruin their project.
Section 3

Epoxy

The strongest structural adhesive in the hobby world

What It Is and How It Works

What it is
A two-part adhesive system — a resin and a hardener sold together. Delivers the strongest, most gap-filling bond of any adhesive in the hobby world.

How it works
Mixing the two parts triggers a chemical curing reaction that creates a cross-linked polymer. The result is extremely hard, waterproof, and resistant to heat and vibration. The bond becomes part of the structure.

How to use
Combine equal parts by volume, mixed thoroughly (undermixing is the #1 failure cause), applied within the working time before it starts to gel. Slower-cure epoxies generally give stronger structural bonds; faster ones are for tacking things in place, but are still stronger than CA.

5-Minute (Quick-Cure)

▾
Fastest cure, lowest final strength. Working time about 2 minutes, handling strength in 5 minutes, full cure in 24 hours. Best for quick repairs and non-structural bonds.

15-Minute (Mid-Cure)

▾
Best balance of working time and strength. Allows repositioning. Best for structural repairs, motor mounts, and firewall reinforcement.

30-Minute (Slow-Cure)

▾
Longest working time, highest final strength. Best for composite layups, wing tube sockets, and any structural RC aircraft joint under real stress. Slower cure always equals stronger bond.

Finishing / Laminating Epoxy

▾
A thin, low-viscosity epoxy used with fiberglass cloth to create a structural laminate skin on foam RC aircraft. Adds rigidity and crash resistance without significant weight. Regular epoxy is too thick for this application. Often referred to as "Glassing"

Mixing Accessories

▾
Sell mixing sticks and disposable mixing cups alongside epoxy. Make the add-on suggestion automatic, a customer buying epoxy without something to mix it with is going to have a bad experience.

Alaska Tip

▾
Cold surfaces reduce bond strength significantly. Recommend customers warm their parts slightly before applying; especially relevant for Alaskan customers doing outdoor RC work, or in a garage during cold months.

Common Uses and Typical Customers

Common uses

  • Structural repairs, motor mounts, firewalls, and reinforcements.
  • Laminating fiberglass over foam wings.
  • Any joint that must survive vibration, impact, or repeated stress.
  • Safe on most surfaces, but very rigid.

Typical customers

  • RC aircraft builders.
  • Any hobbyist where CA alone is not strong enough.
  • Wood workers

Sales note
When a customer describes a structural repair, ask whether they have epoxy, many default to CA out of habit even when epoxy is the right tool.

Section 4

Plastic Cement

A solvent, not a glue — and the distinction matters

What It Is and How It Works

What it is
Not a glue in the traditional sense. Plastic cement is a solvent that melts the surfaces and fuses them into one. There is no bonding layer added between the pieces.

How it works
The solvent softens/melts the plastic surface on both parts, they fuse together as the solvent evaporates, and you end up with what's structurally a single piece of plastic rather than two pieces adhered together.

This only works on polystyrene, it won't bond metal, resin, or most other plastics.

How to use it
Thin/liquid cement (like Tamiya Extra Thin) is applied with a brush after parts are positioned, capillary action pulls it into the seam. Thick/tube cement is applied to one surface before joining, better for gap-filling but messier.

Ventilation matters — these are genuine solvents.

The most important rule in this section
Plastic cement only works on styrene plastic. It will NOT work on resin, polyurethane, ABS, foam, or metal. Always ask what the kit is made of before recommending.

Extra Thin

▾
Ultra-low viscosity. Apply after parts are already pressed together — it wicks into the seam on its own. The go-to for clean, invisible seams on injection-molded kits. Tamiya Extra Thin is the gold standard.

Thin / Regular

▾
Classic bottle cement. Apply to one surface before joining. Slightly more working time and some gap fill. Testors Liquid Cement and Revell Contacta are common in this category.

Thick / Gel

▾
For larger gaps or rougher seams. Apply to one surface, let tack slightly, press together. Best for older kits where parts do not fit cleanly.

Applicators Matter

▾
Many cements include specialized applicators: fine brush caps on Tamiya bottles, needle applicators for precision work, and capillary pipettes. Know which products include which applicators — it is part of the recommendation.

How to Use Extra Thin

▾
Position parts first. Hold together firmly. Touch applicator tip to the seam joint. The cement wicks in on its own. Hold for 30-60 seconds.

How to Use Regular Cement

▾
Brush cement onto one surface. Wait about 30 seconds until slightly tacky. Press parts together and hold. Do not move the joint while it is curing.

Common Uses and Typical Customers

Common uses

  • Building plastic model kits — military, armor, aircraft, automotive, sci-fi.
  • Gundam and other injection-molded figure kits.
  • Any injection-molded styrene kit (Warhammer 40K, other wargaming kits)

Typical customers

  • Scale modelers
  • Gundam builders

Sales note
These customers often come in asking for "model glue." Always ask about the material before recommending. A "standard plastic model" is most likely styrene.

Knowledge Check

A scale modeler asks for the strongest glue you have for their injection-molded plastic military kit. What do you recommend?

Injection-molded military kits are styrene. Plastic cement chemically welds the parts into one piece — that IS the strongest bond for styrene. CA and epoxy sit on top of the surface; cement becomes the surface.
Section 5

Wood Modeling Glues

Traditional adhesives for balsa and basswood construction

What They Are and How They Work

What they are
Adhesives formulated specifically for bonding balsa, basswood, and other lightweight woods used in traditional RC aircraft, model ship, and architectural model construction.

Aliphatic Resin (yellow wood glue)
Water-based PVA-family adhesive. Soaks into wood fibers and bonds at a fiber level as it dries. The water carrier evaporates and the polymer left behind holds the wood — often stronger than the wood itself. Requires clamping or pinning while curing.

Solvent-based wood cement (Testors)
Works faster than aliphatic resin. Better for quick tack during assembly. Stronger initial grab but similar final strength for balsa construction.

A customer is building a traditional balsa-frame RC airplane and needs to glue balsa ribs to a spruce spar. Best product?

Section 6

Other Adhesives

Shoe Goo, Thread Loc, Foam-2-Foam, and Woodland Scenics

Shoe Goo

A thick, flexible contact cement. Apply to both surfaces, let tack a few minutes, then press together. Extremely strong on rubber, vinyl, leather, and canvas. Great for RC body seam bonding and foam bumper attachment. Strong solvent fumes — ventilation required.

BSI Thread Loc — IC-Loc Blue and IC-Loc Red

Thread locker prevents screws and bolts from vibrating loose. IC-Loc Blue (medium strength) is removable with tools — use on RC drivetrain and chassis screws. IC-Loc Red (permanent) is for fasteners you never want to come apart. Not a structural adhesive — it bonds threads, not surfaces. A must-have add-on for every RC car and drone customer.

Deluxe Materials Foam-2-Foam

A water-based contact adhesive formulated specifically for foam-to-foam bonds. Apply to both surfaces, allow to dry, then press together. Safe on all foam types where CA would melt the material. Essential for foam RC aircraft builders and diorama terrain makers.

Woodland Scenics

Scenic Cement — thin PVA in a dropper bottle. The standard method for train layout ballast and ground cover. Dilute with water, apply, dries clear and matte.

Scenic Glue — thick PVA for rocks, trees, and larger scenic elements.

Hob-E-Tac / Foam Tack — foam structure adhesives. Hob-E-Tac allows repositioning before the final bond sets.

Knowledge Check

A customer is building an RC car and complains that chassis screws keep backing out during races. What product do you reach for?

Thread locker is purpose-built to prevent fasteners from vibrating loose. IC-Loc Blue locks the screw but still allows removal with a hex driver. CA on threads can make removal very difficult and may crack under vibration.
